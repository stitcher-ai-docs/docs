**Allocate** is the last Model sub-stage. It distributes shared cost — a Kubernetes platform, an API gateway, an enterprise license billed centrally but used by many — to the teams, products, and departments that consumed it, using **double-entry accounting** so the dataset's totals always reconcile to the bill.

<Note>
  Looking for the hands-on version instead? [How to: Build an allocation](/how-to/build-an-allocation) walks through authoring one rule end to end.
</Note>

## Double-entry accounting

Allocation is where a cost model is most easily broken — move cost carelessly and totals stop matching the bill. StitcherAI prevents that with **double-entry accounting**.

Every allocation posts two kinds of entry:

<Steps>
  <Step title="A balancing negation">
    A negation is posted against the source cost — the central charge is reduced by exactly the amount being moved.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Allocation entries to recipients">
    One or more entries distribute that amount to the teams that consumed the service.
  </Step>
  <Step title="They net to zero">
    The negation and the recipient entries **sum to zero net change**, so the dataset's totals still reconcile to the bill.
  </Step>
</Steps>

The bill total is fixed; allocation only redistributes it. **Nothing is invented or lost — cost is only moved.** Because every move nets to zero, the allocated model reconciles to what providers actually charged, and you can trust the redistributed numbers as readily as the raw bill.

## Multi-level allocations

Real organizations are layered, and allocations chain to match. A shared-services cost is first **attributed** to the shared service (see [Enrich](/model/enrich)), then **allocated** to its consumers — and those consumers may themselves be second-level shared services, allocated onward in turn.

What keeps a chained allocation trustworthy is that every amount carries **allocation status columns**. They record, at each step:

- whether a row is original cost, a negation, or an allocation;
- which **rule** and **ratio** moved it;
- its source-versus-destination status.

So a multi-stage allocation is traceable end to end: you can follow exactly how each dollar flowed from origin to final owner, hop by hop, with no opaque step in between.

## How an allocation rule is expressed

A rule reads like a sentence. You author it in this order:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Scope">
    Which providers or accounts the rule applies to.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Which cost to allocate">
    Filters that select the rows, plus the **grouping** that summarizes them.
  </Step>
  <Step title="How to distribute it">
    A distribution strategy (below).
  </Step>
  <Step title="The balancing negation">
    Posted automatically against the source so the move nets to zero.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Overrides">
    Optional adjustments to the resulting rows.
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Distribution strategies

| Strategy | What it does | When it's relevant |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Fixed** | Distributes by explicit ratios that sum to 100%. | You already know the split. |
| **N-way** | Splits equally across the receivers. | Each receiver shares equally. |
| **Proportionate** | Splits by a driver metric — usage, seats, pod-hours, or revenue. | A usage metric should drive the split. |
| **Replace** | Swaps coarse cost for finer-grained cost from a platform tool, to avoid double-counting. | A more granular source exists for the same resources. |

<Info>
  **Where recipients come from:** an inline table you type, the cost data itself (each distinct group becomes a recipient), or a business dataset.
</Info>

## Worked example: \$100, end to end

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with the providers">
    $100 of cost arrives from two providers — **Cloud Corp ($70)\*\* and **SaaS LLC (\$30)**.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Attribute the shared service">
    **Shared service A** is used by everyone and billed across both providers. It draws \*\*$10 from each provider**, so $20 is first attributed to the shared service.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Allocate the shared service to the apps">
    Shared service A's cost is allocated onward to the apps that consumed it — **App 1** and **App 2** — alongside the cost those apps draw directly from the providers.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Every dollar lands on an app">
    When the moves settle, the full $100 is attributed to apps: **App 1 ($60)\*\* and **App 2 (\$40)**. Each step posts its balancing negation and writes its rule and ratio into the **allocation status columns**, so the path is traceable and the totals still reconcile to the original bill.
  </Step>
</Steps>
