Once your cost model is normalized, attributed, and allocated, the **Generate** step builds specialized outputs on top of it. Those outputs are **artifacts**.

<Note>
  Every artifact has a **group** and a **name** you assign when you create it. That group and name are the handle you use everywhere afterward — to build another artifact from it, to [govern](/cost-governance) it, or to [deliver](/engage) it.
</Note>

## Artifact types

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Indicator / KPI" icon="chart-line">
    A single cost metric tracked over time at a granularity and grouping you choose — effective cost by team per month, or a unit-economics measure like cost per active user. Optionally linked to a budget.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Derived dataset" icon="table">
    A custom view or report built with SQL over the cost model and other artifacts — a chargeback report, margin by feature, or cost per tenant.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Forecast" icon="wand-magic-sparkles" href="/forecasting">
    A projection built on an Indicator. A forecast is itself a generated artifact — see Forecasting.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

### Indicators in depth

An Indicator rolls detailed cost up with an **aggregation** — sum, average, a percentile, distinct count, and so on — at the **granularity** you choose (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, yearly) and the **grouping** you choose (by team, by service, by tenant).

<Tip>
  **Unit economics are just Indicators** whose metric is cost divided by a business quantity — cost per inference, per feature, per tenant. That's what lets you [govern a unit-economics KPI against a threshold](/cost-governance) and what makes [AI unit economics](/managing-ai-spend) possible.
</Tip>

You can optionally link an Indicator to a **budget**. This combines the Indicator with budget figures so you can track **budget versus actual** and the variance between them.

## Artifacts can be chained

An artifact's input can be the primary cost dataset, or another artifact you already generated. You **select that input by its artifact group and name** — the same selection model used by governance and delivery.

<Frame caption="Artifacts can be chained: primary cost → aggregated view → KPI → forecast">
  ![Artifacts can be chained: primary cost → aggregated view → KPI → forecast](/images/fig-11-artifact-chain.png)
</Frame>

So artifacts compose: primary cost becomes an aggregated view, which becomes a KPI, which becomes a forecast. A complex output is assembled from simpler, reusable pieces rather than written as one monolithic query. A chargeback is a separate kind of artifact — a derived dataset built straight from the model, not a step downstream of a KPI.

<Warning>
  Aggregations over large datasets can be expensive. Where the same heavy aggregation feeds several outputs, **do it once as a single artifact, then build the subsequent artifacts from that aggregated result** — instead of re-scanning the full cost data each time. This can materially improve how a pipeline performs.
</Warning>

## Example: a chargeback report

A chargeback is a familiar generated artifact — a **derived dataset / report** that attributes cost to cost centers or teams. Unlike a KPI, which carries a **single cost metric**, a chargeback can carry **both cash and accrual components** in the same report. It's a concrete example of chaining and selection working together.

## Generating is not delivering

<Info>
  **Pushing an artifact out** — handing a chargeback to an ERP, for instance — happens in the [Engage](/engage) phase, to a **custom location**. It is _not_ part of generating the artifact. Generate makes the artifact; Engage delivers it.
</Info>

And because artifacts are computed from the model rather than assembled by hand, they **regenerate live** as new data flows in — every run reflects the latest cost data.
