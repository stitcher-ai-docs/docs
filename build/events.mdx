title: "Events"

---

**Events** define named **event banks** — collections of described business changes and what-ifs (a launch, a migration, a contract change, a past anomaly) that **feed forecasts**. Toggling an event bank on or off lets you compare what-if scenarios side by side.

A forecast is a [generated artifact](/build/generate-artifacts) built on an **Indicator**. You start from a KPI you already track — cost by team, cost per inference, a margin — and the forecast projects that same metric into the future. Anything you can measure, you can forecast.

## Business-driver and event-driven

A StitcherAI forecast reflects what the business is about to do, not just where the line has been. Instead of extrapolating a trend, it incorporates **events**:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Known future changes" icon="calendar-plus">
    Launches, migrations, contract changes — what-ifs you already know are coming.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Past anomalies" icon="triangle-exclamation">
    A one-off spike you don't want repeated in the projection.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Note>
  Events are organized into named **event banks** — a collection of events you can switch on or off as a set. Each event is **authored in plain, natural language**: you describe the change in words, and StitcherAI accounts for it in the forecast.
</Note>

## Explainable

Because the inputs are named, described business events, a forecast explains itself. It's shown **with vs without its events** — two lines, so you can see exactly what the events moved — alongside a plain-language **impact summary** of what changed and why.

> 🖼 **Figure:** A forecast shown with vs without business events, plus an impact summary. _(image: forecast-with-without-events)_

A forecast is explained by business reality, not opaque math.

## What-if scenarios

<Steps>
  <Step title="Toggle an event bank">
    Switch a bank on or off — _with the migration_ versus _without_, _with the launch_ versus _without_.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compare the projections">
    Watch the forecast respond, and compare the futures side by side.
  </Step>
</Steps>

What-if analysis is simply toggling which events apply.

## Improving accuracy

Beyond cost history, a forecast can draw on correlated signals:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Business-driver signals" icon="chart-mixed">
    Headcount, customer count, transaction volume — series that move with cost.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Calendar events" icon="calendar-days">
    Public holidays, fiscal year-end, and other recurring dates.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

These supplementary signals help a forecast track patterns that cost data alone wouldn't reveal.
