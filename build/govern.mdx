title: "Govern"
description: "Evaluate any generated artifact against policy on every run. Control groups run conditions, controls mark pass or fail, and breaches raise alerts routed to the right owner — before the decision is irreversible."
---

**Govern** evaluates the artifacts your model produces and turns a policy breach into a routed alert. A **control group** targets a generated artifact and runs one or more **conditions** against it; the result is a **control** that marks each evaluation pass or fail; and a breach raises an **alert**. It runs on every run, not in a quarterly review — so a breach is caught before the decision it would inform is irreversible.

<Note>
  **Govern to avoid bill-shock.** Because a control can be built on **forecasted data, not just current data**, a breach can surface before the spend lands — not after it shows up on the bill.
</Note>

<Note>
  Governance composes on top of [generated artifacts](/build/generate-artifacts). You select what to govern by its **artifact group and name** — the same way you select an input when chaining artifacts.
</Note>

## How governance works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Build the artifact to govern">
    Govern evaluates a generated artifact — a KPI, a derived dataset, or the primary dataset. Build it first in [Generate](/build/generate-artifacts).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Create a control group">
    Point a control group at that artifact by its **artifact group and name**. The group is what you attach conditions to.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add conditions">
    Add one or more conditions — the policy tests the artifact must satisfy.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Evaluate and alert">
    On every run, the control marks each evaluation pass or fail and raises an **alert** on a breach.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Worked example — a unit-economics KPI against a threshold

Governance is clearest on a unit-economics KPI.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Build a cost-per-active-user KPI">
    In [Generate](/build/generate-artifacts), build a KPI such as **cost per active user** — a unit-economics measure tracked over time.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Govern it with a condition">
    Create a control group targeting that KPI by its artifact group and name, and add a condition.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check it against a threshold">
    The condition checks the KPI against a **defined threshold** — for example, it must stay below a target, or a ratio must hold such as _database cost must be no more than 12% of total IT spend_.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Route the alert to the owner">
    When the KPI crosses the threshold, the control records a failure and an alert is **routed to the owner**.
  </Step>
</Steps>

> 🖼 **Figure:** A control evaluates a unit-economics KPI against a threshold and raises an alert. _(image: kpi-governance-alert)_

<Tip>
  Generate produces the metric, Govern judges it against policy, and a breach reaches a person before it becomes a surprise on the bill.
</Tip>

## Control types

Pick the control type by the shape of the question.

| Control type | What it does | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Row-level control** | A pass/fail verdict **per row**. | A row is missing a required tag, so it fails. |
| **Aggregate-level control** | **One verdict for the whole dataset**. | A total, or a coverage ratio, is checked against a threshold. |

## Conditions and alert routing

Conditions are kept simple — there's no separate policy language to learn.

- A condition is a **column test** (a column compared against a value, including checks for whether a value is present) or a **SQL-style expression**.
- Every condition (rule) carries one or more **tags**. Those tags drive how a breach's alert is **routed** — to the owning team, a channel, or an email group — so the right people hear about the right breach.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Stop at first failure, or evaluate all conditions">
    A control can **stop at the first failed condition** — when one failure is enough to act on — or **evaluate all conditions** in a single pass, when you want the complete picture of every breach at once.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## From breach to action

A failed control raises an alert, and the alert becomes a **notification delivered into the workflows your stakeholders already use** — Slack, Jira, and email — so a breach is acted on where work happens, before the decision it concerns is irreversible.

<Info>
  Alerts are artifacts like any other, so they can also be delivered to a destination in the [Engage](/engage) phase for systems that consume them directly.
</Info>

<Tip>
  Alerts can also trigger **[OpenOps](https://openops.com) hooks** to orchestrate the data into a **custom business workflow you define** — set up in the [Engage](/engage) phase.
</Tip>
